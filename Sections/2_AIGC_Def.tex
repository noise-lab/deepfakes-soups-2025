\section{AI-Generated Content and Its Governance}
\label{sec:def}
We first clarify our definition of AI-generated content and the governance of AI-generated content in this paper, given the lack of a shared consensus on these concepts across existing research and practice.

\subsection{AI-Generated Content}
\label{subsec:AIGC}
\textit{AI-generated content} is a concept originally extended from earlier notions of \textit{computer-generated content} and \textit{machine-generated content}, which refer to media produced by computer systems. While generative networks---the earliest form of generative AI models---were developed in the early 2010s, the term AI-generated content was not widely recognized until 2022-2023, when commercial generative AI products surged. Since then, it has been broadly used to emphasize the generation capabilities of large generative models across modalities such as text, images, audio, and video \cite{cao2025survey, foo2025ai}. 

The term AI-generated content has an evolving scope with ambiguous boundaries across different contexts. Typically, this term denotes content fully generated by generative AI models, as opposed to content made by humans~\cite{cao2025survey}. Yet, with generative AI incorporated at different stages of content creation, whether content is AI-generated is no longer a yes-or-no question but a spectrum of when, where, and how AI participates. Therefore, terms like \textit{AI-assisted content} and \textit{human-AI co-creation} have been created to describe the nuances of partially AI engagement in the content~\cite{lloyd2025ai, 10.1145/3715336.3735683}. However, the term AI-generated content is still widely adopted to encompass a range of AI participation in content creation. For example, this term in platform policies typically refers to content that is fully generated, significantly altered, or whose major components are generated by AI~\cite{gao2026governance}.

In this paper, we adopt a broad working definition of AI-generated content to encompass content fully generated by generative AI models, as well as content produced through AI participation, such as AI-altered, AI-assisted, or human-AI collaborative processes. We also consider deepfakes and synthetic media as AI-generated content as long as their production involves generative AI.


\subsection{Governance of AI-Generated Content}
\label{subsec:govAIGC}
We conceptualize the \textit{governance of AI-generated content} by drawing on two bodies of work: \textit{AI governance} and \textit{platform governance}. AI governance broadly focuses on the life cycle of development, deployment, and oversight of AI systems, emphasizing the breadth of concerns such as safety, accountability, transparency, fairness, and compliance through technical solutions, policymaking, and regulations~\cite{taeihagh2021governance, butcher2019state}. Platform governance encompasses policies, technical mechanisms, and design decisions that affect user interactions in digital platforms, addressing a wide range of concerns like online safety, digital privacy, monetization, and free speech~\cite{gorwa2019platform}. In prior work, platform governance usually refers to the governance \textit{by} platforms---how online platforms govern their ecosystem that bridges user expectations and regulatory requirements. In contrast, the governance \textit{of} platforms incorporates inputs from different parties that shape governance by platforms~\cite{gorwa2019platform, gillespie2017governance}. Unlike AI governance, where governance objects and actors are often problem-specific, digital platforms serve as the center of platform governance, where governance decisions are enacted and encountered directly by their end-users. 

We situate the governance of AI-generated content in online spaces within the intersection of two frameworks. In AI governance, concerns of AI-generated content have become a key focus, covering deepfake~\cite{geng2023comparing}, harmful generative AI output~\cite{gao2025cannot,hacker2023regulating}, and consent and credit for training data~\cite{kyi2025governance}. In platform governance, AI-generated content produced by users has been treated as a subtype of user-generated content. The prevalence of AI-generated content has led to the development of dedicated external regulations and platform policies~\cite{gao2026governance}, indicating AI-generated content as a rising focus in platform governance. 

We thus define the governance of AI-generated content as the collection of regulations, policies, principles, technical mechanisms, and design solutions developed by different stakeholders---such as online platforms, AI service providers, regulators, and researchers---to address the challenges introduced by AI-generated content. While these efforts span the AI supply chain, they are ultimately reflected through governance by platforms: online platforms serve as downstream sites that directly shape end-users' production, presentation, and distribution of AI-generated content.
