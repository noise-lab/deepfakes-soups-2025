\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The rapid development of large generative models and generative AI products has led to a sudden surge of \textit{AI-generated content} in online spaces. This surge has fundamentally altered the scale, visibility, and role of generative AI in online content consumption~\cite{liang2025widespread,matatov2024examining,wei2024understanding,sun2025we,hua2024generative,guo2025exploring}, raising challenges for the \textit{governance of AI-generated content} to maintain trust and safety. While the governance of AI-generated content can be traced back to early AI techniques producing realistic media (i.e., the governance of \textit{deepfakes} and \textit{synthetic media})~\cite{westerlund2019emergence}, the recent proliferation of large models marks a departure from the conditions where such governance initially took shape. In this paper, we revisit the development of AI-generated content governance and explore future paradigms better suited to the current landscape.

Stakeholders have already devoted substantial efforts to governing AI-generated content. Yet current approaches remain largely incident-based, targeting explicit harms: moderating AI-generated content that maps to established online harm categories, under a clear threat model of attacker, victim, harm, and consequence. For example, in general-purpose generative AI products, governance primarily focuses on preventing harmful output and service abuse, such as prohibiting the generation of disturbing, privacy-violating, and explicit content~\cite{gao2025cannot,riccio2024exploring,klyman2024acceptable}. On online platforms such as social media, governance mainly focuses on enforcing existing community guidelines and labeling AI-generated content for authenticity. Few platforms have made new rules on unique challenges of AI-generated content like ownership and monetization~\cite{gao2026governance}, which are often addressed through user-driven, bottom-up efforts~\cite{lloyd2025there,lloyd2025ai}. Where new policies do exist, they are largely incident-based and one-cut restrictive, such as a complete ban on monetizing AI-generated content~\cite{gao2026governance}.

We argue that the current incident-based governance targeting explicit harms, while essential, may be insufficient as generative AI shifts from a tool primarily exploited by bad actors to one routinely used by everyday users. Many consequential effects of AI-generated content surges are diffuse, gradual, and difficult to delineate, accumulating into negative impacts and eventually compromising trust and safety. For example, high-volume, low-effort AI-generated posts (``AI slop'') can erode perceived information value and credibility at the community level, even when individual items are not harmful~\cite{Gross2025AISlopAP, mahdawi2025slop, hoffman2024first}. The widespread use of generative AI complicates ownership, authorship, and originality of creative works~\cite{10.1145/3706598.3713522,kyi2025governance}. Such controversies pose challenges for online platforms in allocating credit for algorithmic promotion and monetization~\cite{cosen2024new}. As AI-mediated communication---communicators adopt AI to modify, augment, or generate messages for communication goals~\cite{hancock2020ai}---becomes common, the boundaries between AI-generated and human-made content blur, reforming perceptions of content authenticity and value~\cite{Gross2025AISlopAP, eissa2025influence}. These emerging challenges, though often not causing immediate damage, could eventually compromise user experiences, creator rights, and community values if not well-addressed. They are either not covered by the current governance targets of explicit harms, or cannot be fully addressed through incident-based logic. 

Therefore, we suggest that the future governance of AI-generated content should extend from incident-based, explicit harm mitigation to address its broader socio-technical and epistemic impacts on the online information ecosystem. We begin by clarifying the definition of AI-generated content and its governance in this paper. Then, we synthesize the development of the current governance landscape across regulation, policy, technical solutions, and research. After that, we discuss why governance of AI-generated content should extend beyond an incident-based framework focused on mitigating explicit harms. We finally outline recommendations for online platforms, AI developers, regulators, and researchers to improve the future governance of AI-generated content in online spaces.

Our paper contributes to AI governance and trust and safety by providing an overview of current AI-generated content governance and why it falls short of addressing the holistic shifts it is making in the online information ecosystem. In addition, we contribute concrete recommendations for researchers, policymakers, and industry practitioners on how best to get ahead of this shift as generative AI continues to evolve.
